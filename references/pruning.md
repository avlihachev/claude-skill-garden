# Pruning Reference

## Critical rule: timing by fruit type

| Type | When to prune | Why |
|------|--------------|-----|
| **Pome fruit** (apple, pear) | Late winter / early spring, before bud break | Wounds heal well, no disease risk |
| **Stone fruit** (cherry, plum) | **Summer only** (July-August) | Spring pruning → silver leaf disease (Chondrostereum purpureum), bacterial canker |
| **Berry bushes** (currants, gooseberry) | Late winter / early spring | Before sap flow, easy to see structure |
| **Cane fruit** (raspberry, blackberry) | Early spring | Remove spent canes, tip new growth |
| **Sea buckthorn** | Early spring | Remove dead/damaged only |

**Exception for all types:** dead and broken branches can be removed at any time.

## Apple trees

### Annual maintenance
- Remove dead, diseased, crossing branches
- Remove water sprouts (vertical shoots from main branches)
- Thin center for light penetration and air circulation
- Cut to an outward-facing bud to encourage open shape

### Old/neglected trees
- Never remove more than 25-30% of canopy in one year
- Spread renovation over 2-3 years
- Prioritize: dead wood → crossing branches → water sprouts → thinning

### Young trees (1-5 years)
- Formative pruning: establish central leader
- Remove competing leaders
- Select 3-4 well-spaced scaffold branches
- Minimal pruning — don't over-cut while establishing

### Cuts
- Cuts > 3 cm: seal with wound paint / pruning wax
- Cut flush with branch collar, don't leave stubs
- Use sharp, clean tools (disinfect between trees if disease present)

## Pear trees

Same principles as apple, but:
- More upright growth habit — may need more thinning of vertical growth
- Young trees: even less aggressive — pears are slow to establish

## Cherry trees

### SUMMER PRUNING ONLY (July-August)
- Wounds heal fastest in active growth
- Fungal spore load is lowest
- Sap flow direction helps seal wounds

### What to prune
- Dead and diseased wood
- Crossing branches
- Reduce height if needed (cherries can grow very tall)
- Thin interior for light

### Old cherry trees
- Same 25-30% rule as apple
- Consider if rejuvenation is worth it vs. replacement

## Plum trees

### SUMMER PRUNING ONLY (July-August)
- Same reasoning as cherry — silver leaf risk
- Plums sucker from roots — remove suckers as they appear (any time of year)

### Shape
- Open center / vase shape preferred
- Remove inward-growing branches
- Keep height manageable for harvest

## Currants (red, white, black)

### Red and white currant
- Fruit on 2-3 year old wood
- Remove branches **older than 4 years** (thick, dark bark, reduced fruiting)
- Keep 8-12 branches of mixed ages (some 1yr, some 2yr, some 3yr)
- Thin center for air circulation

### Black currant
- Fruit best on **1-2 year old wood**
- More aggressive pruning — remove branches **older than 3 years**
- Keep 6-8 strong branches
- New shoots from base are desired — don't remove them

## Gooseberry

- Similar to red currant
- Remove branches older than 4 years
- Keep 8-10 branches
- **Thin center aggressively** — air circulation prevents powdery mildew
- Remove low-hanging branches touching ground (fruit gets dirty, disease)

## Raspberry

### Summer-bearing (most common)
- Fruits on **second-year canes** (floricanes)
- After harvest: cut spent canes to ground
- In spring: tip first-year canes to ~150 cm to encourage side branching
- Thin to 6-8 canes per running meter

### Everbearing / autumn-bearing
- Can cut all canes to ground in late winter for one autumn crop
- Or manage as two-crop system: keep some for summer, cut others

## Blackberry

- Fruits on **second-year canes** (like summer raspberry)
- Early spring: remove spent fruiting canes (brown, often dying)
- Keep new primocanes and tie to support
- Tip primocanes at 150-180 cm to encourage branching

## Sea buckthorn

- Minimal pruning needed
- Remove dead, damaged, diseased branches in early spring
- **Do not prune male trees aggressively** — needed for pollination
- Ideal ratio: 1 male per 4-6 females, within 10-15 m
