# Soil and Substrates Reference

## Substrate types

| Type | Use case | Characteristics |
|------|----------|----------------|
| Seed-starting mix | Sowing, germination | Fine texture, low nutrients, sterile, good moisture retention |
| Potting mix (seedling) | Pricking out, young plants | Medium nutrients, good drainage |
| Universal potting soil | Adult plants in containers | Higher nutrients, coarser texture |
| Vegetable/tomato soil | Fruiting crops | Enriched with nutrients, good water retention |

## Components

| Component | Purpose | Typical ratio |
|-----------|---------|---------------|
| Peat | Moisture retention, acidity | 30-60% of mix |
| Coconut coir | Peat alternative, moisture retention | 30-50% (replaces peat) |
| Perlite | Drainage, aeration | 15-30% |
| Vermiculite | Moisture + nutrient retention | 10-20% |
| Compost | Nutrients, biology | 10-30% (not for seed starting) |
| Sand (coarse) | Drainage, weight | 5-15% |
| Worm castings | Gentle fertilizer, biology | 5-15% |

## Recipes by use case

### Seed starting
- 70% seed-starting mix + 30% perlite
- Or: 50% peat/coir + 30% perlite + 20% vermiculite
- No compost, no fertilizer — seeds have their own reserves

### Seedlings (after pricking out)
- 60% potting mix + 20% perlite + 20% compost
- Or commercial seedling/potting soil as-is

### Adult plants in containers
- 50% universal potting soil + 20% compost + 20% perlite + 10% vermiculite
- Or: commercial vegetable soil + 20-30% perlite for drainage
- Add slow-release fertilizer granules at planting

### Herbs
- 60% universal soil + 30% perlite + 10% sand
- Herbs prefer lighter, well-drained soil with moderate fertility

## pH ranges

| Crop | Preferred pH |
|------|-------------|
| Tomatoes | 6.0-6.8 |
| Peppers | 6.0-6.8 |
| Basil | 6.0-7.0 |
| Herbs (most) | 6.0-7.0 |
| Blueberries | 4.5-5.5 |
| Strawberries | 5.5-6.5 |
| Most vegetables | 6.0-7.0 |

Most commercial potting soils are pH 5.5-6.5 — suitable for the majority of crops without adjustment.

## Substrate management in containers

### When to replace
- Annual crops: fresh soil each season (old soil loses structure and nutrients)
- Perennials: top-dress annually, full replacement every 2-3 years
- If disease occurred: discard soil, don't reuse

### Common problems

| Problem | Cause | Fix |
|---------|-------|-----|
| Compacted soil, poor drainage | Peat breakdown over time | Add perlite, replace soil |
| White crust on surface | Salt buildup from fertilizer | Flush with clean water, reduce feeding |
| Hydrophobic soil (water runs off) | Dried-out peat | Soak container in basin, add wetting agent |
| Fungus gnats | Overly moist surface, organic matter | Let top layer dry between waterings, use sand mulch |
